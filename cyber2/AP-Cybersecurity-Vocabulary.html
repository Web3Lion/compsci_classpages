AP Cybersecurity · Effective Fall 2026

Vocabulary by Module

126 terms drawn from the AP Cybersecurity Course & Exam Description, organized by course module. Mr. Reasey.

01 Threats, Adversaries & Attacks 12 terms

Social Engineering
Using psychological tricks to manipulate people into revealing information, downloading a malicious file, or clicking a malicious link.
Phishing
A social engineering attack, usually by email or message, that tricks a target into giving up information or clicking a malicious link.
Pretexting
Creating a believable fake reason to contact a target in order to gain their trust.
Elicitation
Manipulating someone into revealing sensitive information.
Adversary
A person or group that carries out a cyberattack.
Script Kiddie
A low-skilled adversary who uses tools built by others without understanding how they work.
Hacktivist
An adversary motivated by social, political, or personal causes.
Insider Threat
An adversary who already has legitimate credentials and access to an organization's systems and data.
Zero-Day
An undocumented vulnerability that has no patch available yet.
Reconnaissance
The attack phase in which adversaries gather as much information as possible about a target.
Open Source Intelligence (OSINT)
Freely available public information that adversaries collect about a target.
Malware
Malicious software that damages a device or network, or gives an adversary access to it.

02 Organizational Security 14 terms

Confidentiality
Ensuring that only authorized individuals, systems, or processes can access data.
Integrity
Ensuring that data is accurate and trustworthy and has not been altered.
Availability
Ensuring that data and services are accessible to authorized users when needed.
Asset
Anything of value to an organization, such as data, money, infrastructure, or reputation.
Risk
The possibility that a threat exploits a vulnerability to compromise an asset.
Risk Assessment
Evaluating the likelihood and severity of possible attacks against vulnerabilities.
Risk Mitigation
Implementing security controls to reduce the likelihood or impact of a risk.
Residual Risk
The risk that remains after an organization applies avoidance, transference, and mitigation.
Security Control
A safeguard that protects systems and data; it can be physical, technical, or managerial.
Managerial Control
Rules, policies, and procedures that specify the security that should be in place.
Defense in Depth
A layered security strategy using multiple controls so that if one fails, others still protect.
Preventative Control
A control that stops an adversary from attacking, such as a lock or encryption.
Detective Control
A control that helps identify attacks when they occur, such as an IDS or camera.
Corrective Control
A control that fixes problems and restores systems, such as patching or an IPS.

03 Physical Security 12 terms

Piggybacking
Using social engineering to get an authorized person to grant access to a restricted area.
Tailgating
Following closely behind an authorized person into a restricted area without their knowledge.
Shoulder Surfing
Watching a user access sensitive information so the adversary can use it later.
Dumpster Diving
Searching through a target's physical trash for information useful to an attack.
Card Cloning
Copying an authorized user's access card to gain the access that user has.
Access Control Vestibule
A two-door entry (mantrap) that prevents an unauthorized person from following someone into a restricted area.
Bollard
A sturdy post that blocks vehicles from entering a restricted area.
Card Reader
A device that records and controls which badges are used to access entries and denies unauthorized badges.
Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS)
A backup power source that keeps a device running during a power outage.
Security Awareness Training
Educating employees so they can recognize and help prevent security threats.
Clean Desk Policy
A policy requiring sensitive documents be cleared from workstations when left unattended.
Motion Sensor
A detective control that alerts security to movement in an area.

04 Network Attacks & Vulnerabilities 13 terms

ARP Poisoning
Sending falsified ARP packets so the adversary's device receives traffic intended for the target.
MAC Spoofing
Faking a device's MAC address to impersonate a legitimate device.
MAC Flooding
Overwhelming a switch with many fake MAC addresses to force it to broadcast traffic.
On-Path Attack (Man-in-the-Middle)
Secretly intercepting and relaying communication between two parties who think they talk directly.
Eavesdropping (Sniffing)
Capturing and copying data while it is in transit on a network.
DNS Poisoning
Planting a fake DNS record to redirect users' browsers to a malicious website.
Credential Harvesting
Using a fake login page that looks real to capture users' credentials.
Denial of Service (DoS)
An attack that makes a system or resource unavailable to authorized users.
Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)
A denial of service attack launched from many devices at the same time.
Rogue Access Point
An unauthorized wireless access point plugged into a network to gain access to it.
Evil Twin
A fake wireless access point that imitates a legitimate one to capture victims' traffic.
Jamming
Flooding an area with a strong signal in the network's frequency to block wireless traffic.
War Driving
Moving around a target to detect wireless network signals and gather information about them.

05 Detecting & Protecting Network Attacks 14 terms

Firewall
Software, on a device or router, that allows or denies network traffic based on rules.
Stateless Firewall
A firewall that filters traffic using only packet header information like IPs, ports, and protocols.
Stateful Firewall
A firewall that tracks the state of connections and can filter by connection-related rules.
Next-Generation Firewall (NGFW)
A firewall with advanced features like intrusion prevention and deep packet inspection.
Access Control List (ACL)
The ordered set of rules a firewall uses to permit or deny inbound and outbound traffic.
Network Segmentation
Dividing a network into smaller isolated subnets to limit how far an attack can spread.
Screened Subnet (DMZ)
A network segment between the internet and internal network that holds public-facing resources.
VLAN
A logical grouping that separates devices that are physically connected to the same switches.
Port Security
Limiting the number of MAC addresses allowed on a switch port to prevent MAC flooding.
WPA3
Currently the strongest wireless encryption protocol.
VPN
An encrypted tunnel that protects a user's traffic over a public network.
Network Intrusion Detection System (NIDS)
A tool that analyzes network data for malicious activity and raises an alert.
Network Intrusion Prevention System (NIPS)
A tool that detects malicious network activity and can also block or stop it.
SIEM
A system that collects and analyzes data from many sources to detect attacks and raise alerts.

06 Device & Password Vulnerabilities & Attacks 14 terms

Virus
Malware that must be activated by a user executing or opening a file.
Worm
Malware that spreads from one computer to another without human interaction.
Trojan
Malware embedded in other software that appears harmless.
Ransomware
Malware that encrypts a device's files and demands payment for the decryption key.
Spyware
Malware that tracks a user's actions and sends the information back to an adversary.
Keylogger
Software or hardware that records a user's keystrokes and reports them to an adversary.
Rootkit
Sophisticated malware that controls the operating system and hides itself from detection.
Fileless Malware
Malicious code that lives in RAM and uses legitimate installed programs to compromise a device.
Internet of Things (IoT)
Everyday devices with embedded computers that connect to the internet.
Brute Force Attack
Using an automated tool to test every possible password a user could have.
Dictionary Attack
Using an automated tool to test a list of common passwords.
Rainbow Table Attack
Using a precomputed table of passwords and their hashes to crack a captured hash.
Password Spraying
Trying a few common passwords against many different user accounts.
Credential Stuffing
Using stolen or default username and password pairs to break into accounts.

07 Detecting & Protecting from Device & Password Attacks 12 terms

Hashing
Converting data into a fixed-length, irreversible value called a hash, checksum, or digest.
Collision
When two different inputs produce the same hash output.
Salt
Random bits added to a password before hashing so identical passwords get different hashes.
Multifactor Authentication (MFA)
Requiring two or more independent factors to verify a user's identity.
Authentication Factor
A category of proof of identity: something you know, have, are, or somewhere you are.
Biometric Factor
Authentication that measures a physical trait such as a fingerprint, face, or iris.
Anti-Malware Software
Software that scans for, quarantines, and removes malware using known signatures.
Signature
A detectable indicator that identifies a specific piece of malware.
Patch
A small update that fixes a known vulnerability in software.
Host-Based Firewall
A firewall that runs on an individual device to filter its traffic.
Account Lockout
Disabling an account after a set number of failed login attempts.
Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)
Signs in logs or systems that suggest a cyberattack has occurred.

08 Application & Data Attacks & Vulnerabilities 8 terms

Data Validation
Verifying that user input meets expected criteria before an application processes it.
Injection Attack
Inserting unexpected character strings into input fields to alter a program's behavior.
SQL Injection
Placing SQL commands into an input field to read, change, or delete database data.
Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)
Injecting malicious code into a website that a user's browser then executes.
Buffer
A fixed-size section of computer memory that holds input data.
Buffer Overflow
Feeding more data into memory than allotted, which can crash a system or run malicious code.
Directory Traversal
Modifying URLs or requests to access files outside a web application's intended folder.
Privilege Escalation
Gaining a higher level of access than authorized, often by compromising an admin account.

09 Cryptography 15 terms

Cryptography
The practice of hiding information so that only authorized parties can read it.
Encryption
The process of converting readable information into an unreadable form.
Decryption
Reversing encryption to retrieve the original information.
Plaintext
The original readable information before it is encrypted.
Ciphertext
The unreadable output produced by an encryption algorithm.
Key
The secret value that is combined with information to encrypt or decrypt it.
Keyspace
The total number of possible keys; a larger keyspace is harder for an adversary to crack.
Symmetric Encryption
Encryption that uses the same key to both encrypt and decrypt information.
Asymmetric Encryption
Encryption that uses a public/private key pair, one key to encrypt and the other to decrypt.
Public Key
The freely published key in an asymmetric key pair, used to encrypt data to its owner.
Private Key
The secret key in an asymmetric key pair that must be stored securely to decrypt data.
AES
The Advanced Encryption Standard, the most common symmetric encryption algorithm.
RSA
A common asymmetric encryption algorithm used for secure communication and signatures.
Digital Signature
Cryptographic proof that a message is authentic and has not been altered.
Block Encryption
Encryption that processes data in fixed-size chunks called blocks.

10 Detecting & Protecting from Application & Data Attacks 12 terms

Access Control
Enforcing which subjects (users or apps) can access, modify, or remove which objects.
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
Granting access based on a user's assigned role in the organization.
Rule-Based Access Control (RuBAC)
Granting or denying access based on a set of rules, such as time of day.
Discretionary Access Control (DAC)
Letting the owner of a resource decide what access other users have to it.
Mandatory Access Control (MAC)
Strict access based on levels assigned by an administrator, as in the Bell-LaPadula model.
Principle of Least Privilege
Giving an entity only as much access as it needs to do its job and no more.
Data States
The three states of data — at rest (stored), in transit (moving), and in use (being processed) — each needing different protection.
Personally Identifiable Information (PII)
Any data that can identify a person, such as name, SSN, address, or biometrics.
Protected Health Information (PHI)
Health-related data protected under HIPAA, such as test results and treatment records.
Input Sanitization
Cleaning user input to remove characters or code that could trigger an attack.
Secure by Design
Building security into every phase of product development, not just as an add-on feature.
Secure by Default
Shipping products with their security features already enabled out of the box.
